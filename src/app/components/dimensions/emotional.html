Definition of Emotional:

"A complex pattern of changes, including physiological arousal, feelings, cognitive processes, and behavioral reactions, made in response to a situation perceived to be personally significant." American Psychological Association

"A strong feeling deriving from one's circumstances, mood or relationships with others;
instinctive or intuitive feeling as distinguished from reasoning or knowledge." - Oxford Dictionaries

TFL Self-Guided Exercises:

Take time to think about the following questions and perhaps answer them in a personal Ideal Being Journal that you can go back and reference and add to over time. We encourage you to share and discuss your answers with a coach, counselor or friend. You will be asked to answer the same seven questions for each dimension and we encourage you to take the time to do this self-reflective exercise. Your answers will give you greater clarity and a good foundation in which to begin your journey to a state of Ideal Being.

  1. What does your life currently look and feel like in this dimension?
  2. Specifically what are the things you are doing to feel satisfied in this dimension of your life?
  3. What do you envision your life to look and feel like if you gain greater satisfaction in this dimension?
  4. Specifically what would you like to integrate into your life in this dimension?
  5. What are the barriers that are keeping you from improving this dimension of your life?
  6. What can you do to eliminate or navigate around these barriers?
  7. What tools and support do you need to be successful?

Self-awareness Exercises:

  1. Accentuate the positive exercise: Don’t wait until negative emotions wake you up to how you are feeling. Be proactive to pause and connect when you are feeling emotions like joy, peace and happiness. Make it a point to sit down and connect to your thoughts and let what you are thinking move into your body and connect to what you are feeling. Sit still and notice things like your breath, heartrate, skin temperature and sensations in your body. Let the moment imprint on your mind and body and take an extra moment to be grateful. This exercise will help you build gratitude muscles so when life gets hard you will be better equipped to deal with its challenges. Consider staring a gratitude journal and record your positive experiences. Journaling helps rewire our thinking and can reinforce new positive behavior.
  2. Power in the pause exercise: Life circumstances can trigger emotions like fear, anxiety and sadness. These are all normal emotions, but the better equipped we are to handle them the better we can manage our emotions in a healthy manner. It starts with awareness, so when you notice you are feeling anxious or overwhelmed, don’t disregard your emotions. Stop what you are doing and sit down in order to connect to what is happening. Give yourself a moment to gain your emotional equilibrium and take 10 slow deep breaths. Connect to your thoughts and let what you are thinking move into your body and connect to what you are feeling. Sit still and notice things like your breath, heartrate and skin temperature. Just the exercise of slowing down, breathing and connecting to your body will help your emotions settle down. Once you have taken these steps try and detach from your emotional reaction and begin looking at the situation more carefully to better understand the root cause and why you have been emotionally triggered. When we can pause, step back and become a witness to our own lives, we can often see things more clearly and objectively. Consider journaling if you encounter a situation that triggers you. It will help you make sense of your emotions, pinpoint patterns of behavior and gain clarity. Research has shown that journaling helps reduce stress, improve problem solving and it can even improve your health.
  3. Define your feelings exercise: Give your feelings Defining your feelings is also important in managing emotions. One activity to help you reflect on your feelings and better understand them is to complete the following sentences:

    1. If this feeling was a color, it would be
    2. If this feeling was weather, it would be
    3. If this feeling was a landscape, it would be
    4. If this feeling was music, it would sound like
    5. If this feeling was an object, it would be

These exercises help to sharpen your awareness, so you can notice even subtle signs of feelings. The earlier you can define an emotion, the sooner you can know how to handle it most effectively (1).

Emotions are like a kaleidoscope, forever changing with new patterns of behavior, feelings and reactions.  Becoming more aware and awake to your feelings will allow you to take greater control of your life and your happiness.

Did You Know:

As humans, we experience an array of emotions, from happiness, to sadness to extreme joy and depression.  Different feelings are experienced within the body as a result of chemicals released specific to each emotion (1).  There is a direct connection between our bodies’ physical and emotional responses. When you have a pain or tightness in an area of your body, it may be related to something you are feeling emotionally (1).  Poor emotional health can weaken your body’s immune system, making you more likely to get colds and other infections during emotionally difficult times (2).  While the following symptoms can indicate the presence of other serious illnesses (and as such should be discussed with your health care provider), they can also be signs that your emotional health is out of balance:

  1. Back pain
  2. Change in appetite
  3. Chest pain
  4. Digestive issues
  5. Dry mouth
  6. Headaches
  7. Heart palpitations
  8. Lightheadedness
  9. Sexual problems
  10. Sleep issues
  11. Sweating
  12. Weight change

People who are in good emotional health are aware of their thoughts, feelings and behaviors. They have learned healthy ways to cope with the stress and problems that are a normal part of life.  They feel good about themselves and have healthy relationships (2).”
Once you are conscious of your thought patterns and emotions, you can begin to see the relationship between emotional and physical pain. This gives you greater control over your body; physical conditions can improve or even go away once you are better attuned to them (1).
Remember, the power to get through anything life throws at you lies within you (1).

TFL Actions to Consider:

  1. Recognize that emotions can affect your health and well-being.
  2. Get in the habit of taking note of at least 3 good experiences you have each day for which you are grateful.
  3. Try meditating or going to a yoga or fitness class.  Being mindful and exercising can help lighten your mood and reinforce positive emotions.
  4. Recognize your emotions and understand why you are having them.  Sorting out the causes of sadness, stress and anxiety in your life can help you manage your emotional health.
  5. Express your feelings in constructive and appropriate ways.
  6. Listen to music or a guided imagery CD to help control negative emotions.
  7. Develop resilience by having social support, viewing yourself positively, accepting change and keeping things in perspective.
  8. You are what you eat!  Eat healthy meals that nourish your body and mind.
  9. Get the proper amount of sleep.  Your mind and body need rest to repair and recharge.
  10. Seek professional help if you turn to drugs, alcohol or other unhealthy behavior to cope with emotional stress or depression. 
  11. See your doctor regularly to ensure you are keeping up with your overall physical and mental well-being.  
  12. Ease worry and stress by proactively managing circumstances and events that you can control.
  13. Don’t hesitate to talk to a trusted friend or family member if you need help.  
  14. Try behavioral counseling; seeking help is a sign of strength not weakness. 
  15. Take note of the things that bring you joy and happiness and do them.
  16. Recognize that experiencing positive and negative emotions are both important. It is what makes human beings unique. 
  17. Don’t hold emotions inside; let them out in a way that is healthy for you and other people.
  18. Become aware of things that trigger your negative emotions and develop a game plan to deal with or avoid these types of situations.
  19. Notice negative self-talk and replace it with positive words of affirmation.

TFL Tips

Negative emotions, like rage, envy or bitterness, tend to spiral out of control, especially right after they’ve been triggered.   Instead of simply reacting, slow things down and observe.  You will find you have the tools to process emotions and illness quickly when you see them for what they are and explore why they came up (1).”

Here are six steps to help control your emotions in a challenging situation (2):

Don’t react right away:  Take a deep breath.  Continue to breathe deeply for five minutes, noticing as your muscles become less tense and your heart rate returns to normal.  As you become calmer, affirm to yourself that this is only temporary. 
Ask for divine guidance:  Faith is our saving grace in our darkest moments.  No matter your creed, when burdened with emotion, close your eyes, envision a positive solution to your problem, and ask the universe to illuminate the best path forward.
Find a healthy outlet:  Call or go see someone you trust and recount to them what happened.  Engage in journaling, meditation, exercise or another healing activity.
See the bigger picture:  Even in the midst of an emotionally upsetting moment, trust that there exists an ultimate purpose which you will come to comprehend soon.
Replace your thoughts:  Whenever you are confronted with a negative emotion, force bad thoughts out of your mind and replace them with different ones.  Imagine the ideal resolution to your problem playing out; think about someone or something that makes you happy.
Forgive your emotional triggers:  When you forgive someone who stirs negative emotions in you, you detach from resentment, jealousy or fury.
Seek immediate professional help: If you are suffering from chronic stress, depression or anxiety and feel you are a danger to yourself or others.

If you continue to suffer from the effects of emotional distress and feel overwhelmed by it, you should contact a professional.  Here are some red flags to look out for:

  1. Inability to sleep
  2. Feeling down, hopeless or helpless most of the time
  3. Problems with your ability to concentrate that are interfering with your work or home life
  4. Using alcohol, drugs, food or tobacco to cope with difficult emotions
  5. Negative or self-destructive thoughts or fears that you can't control*
  6. Thoughts of death or suicide*

* Having self-destructive behavior or thoughts, especially suicidal ones, is a symptom that needs immediate attention.  If you experience such feelings and feel that you need help, contact EAP if your company provides this service or the National Suicide Prevention Lifeline’s toll-free number, which is available 24 hours every day of the year: 1-800-273-TALK (8255).  This service is available to everyone.  You may call for yourself or for someone you care about.  All calls are confidential.
